Yesterday, I sat down to watch the live launch of Artemis 2. For anyone who might have missed the headlines *(which, as I’ve realized, seems to be most people)*, this is the NASA mission carrying four astronauts to orbit the Moon. It is the first time humans are returning to lunar proximity in over 50 years.

Watching the engines ignite, I felt that familiar mix of awe and adrenaline. But as the rocket cleared the tower, my mind started drifting toward some uncomfortable reflections on where we are today.

---

### The Stakes of Intelligence
As someone who works with technology, I couldn't help but think about Artificial Intelligence. Currently, I use AI tools, whether it’s GitHub Copilot, Claude, or Antigravity, to help me write code.

If the AI makes a mistake in my script, the worst-case scenario is a bug I have to spend twenty minutes fixing. *(I say this as if those twenty minutes don't occasionally turn into three hours of questioning my life choices)*. But in the context of Artemis, a "hallucination" or a minor calculation error isn't just a technical glitch. It’s a catastrophe.

A disaster in a mission like this doesn't just impact the lives of four brave astronauts; it stunts the momentum of human exploration for a generation. It’s hard for me to see how AI, in its current "black box" state, can be fully integrated into these high-stakes fields. We aren't just talking about efficiency; we are talking about the margin between history and tragedy.

---

### Why Doesn't Anyone Care?
What struck me even more than the launch itself was the silence following it. I looked around, talked to peers, and checked social feeds, only to realize that most people my age didn't even know it was happening. And even worse? Once they knew, they didn't seem to care.

I think back to 1969 *(I wasn't even born, my dad was 2 years old, I'm just guessing ahah)*. You didn't have to be a "space enthusiast" to be glued to the TV when Apollo 11 landed. People understood that a page of history was being written in real-time. Yesterday, we were writing a new chapter, yet the stadium was half-empty.

*(To be fair, I’m not exactly a "space nerd" myself. Though I wouldn't say no to a job offer from NASA or ESA if they're reading this. Call me?)*

The reality is that we are being buried under a mountain of "Brain Rot" and "AI Slop". Our brains are being conditioned by TikTok, Reels and endless scrolling. If a piece of information requires more than 15 seconds of sustained attention to process, we skip it. We spend hours "doom-scrolling" through content we will forget 10 seconds later, while the actual future of our species is unfolding on another tab, ignored.

---

### A Disconnected Generation
I want to be clear: I’m not judging from a pedestal. *(I am just as guilty of losing hours to a mindless feed and feeling like a husk of a human being afterward)*. But I do think there is a vital difference between participating in the "slop" and being consumed by it.

Many people I spoke to had a reaction along the lines of: "Ah, cool. Space. Whatever". They failed to see how this impacts the future of humanity. We’ve lost the ability to recognize what is truly important because we are too busy chasing the next dopamine hit from a 10-second clip.

Artemis 2 is a bridge to the future, to Mars, to permanent lunar bases, to things we used to only see in movies. It’s a shame that so many people are missing the view because they can't look up from their screens.

I’ve always been interested in how social media has changed the way we live *(usually for the worse)*. But I hadn't truly realized how tangible this change is in our everyday lives until I witnessed this collective disinterest.

These are just some raw thoughts I had today while reflecting on the launch, mostly derived from my friends reactions, maybe *(I hope)* the world isn't all like this, but it's what I've seen. This blog isn't about "perfect" content anyway; it's more about capturing these moments of clarity before they get lost in the noise.
